The Most Advertised Treatment Isn’t Always the Most Effective

Jiro Amato

Hair Loss is Common

Hair loss is incredibly common. Twenty-five percent of men start experiencing some hair loss before age 21, and two-thirds of men will notice significant hair loss before 35. For women, about half will experience hair loss over their lifetime. With so many people affected, it’s no surprise that the hair loss treatment industry is estimated to be worth three billion dollars today.

What You See Isn’t What Works Best

Most Prominent ≠ Most Effective

Walk into any pharmacy and you’ll see shelves full of hair loss treatments. The most prominent product you’ll find is minoxidil, commonly known as Rogaine. It’s over-the-counter, heavily advertised, and what most people reach for when they notice their hair thinning. But here’s the thing: despite its popularity, minoxidil is not the most effective treatment available.

The Evidence: Treatment Rankings

A 2022 study published in JAMA Dermatology used a method called network meta-analysis to compare hair loss treatments. The researchers combined data from 23 clinical trials to rank treatments by effectiveness.

The results? Dutasteride ranked first with an effectiveness score of 96%. Finasteride came in second at around 88%. And minoxidil? It ranked third with a score of about 52%.

So why the difference? Pattern baldness is primarily driven by a hormone called DHT. Dutasteride reduces DHT levels by about 92%, and finasteride reduces it by about 73%. Minoxidil, however, works differently. It increases blood flow to hair follicles, which helps, but it doesn’t address the root cause of the problem.

How Do We Know? Network Meta-Analysis

What makes this study particularly interesting from a data science perspective is the use of network meta-analysis. This technique allows researchers to compare treatments even when they weren’t tested directly against each other in the same trial. By building a network of all available studies, the method estimates how each treatment compares to every other one. It’s a powerful way to synthesize evidence when direct head-to-head comparisons don’t exist.

The Takeaway

Accessibility ≠ Effectiveness

Talk to your doctor about all options

So why is the less effective treatment more heavily advertised? Accessibility. Minoxidil is available over-the-counter, so companies can market directly to consumers. Dutasteride and finasteride require prescriptions, which means less direct advertising and less consumer awareness.

The takeaway is simple: the products you see advertised most aren’t necessarily the most effective. If you’re serious about results, it’s worth having a conversation with a doctor about all your options, including prescription treatments.
